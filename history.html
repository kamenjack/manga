The History of Japanese Manga

The history of Japanese manga can be traced back to pre-World War II visual storytelling traditions. Works from the Edo period, such as ukiyo-e and kibyōshi, told stories through images, blending humor, satire, and social commentary, and laid the visual and narrative foundations for later manga.

Modern manga, in the sense we understand today, truly emerged after World War II. In the 1950s, manga artist Osamu Tezuka pioneered the modern manga style. His use of black-and-white printing for easier reproduction, cinematic paneling for smoother storytelling, and Disney-inspired cartoon characters made his works stand out. Among these innovations, the concepts of cinematic storytelling and black-and-white illustration became core principles of Japanese manga.

Osamu Tezuka’s Shin Takarajima, the origin of modern manga

Manga in the 1950s was primarily considered children’s literature. As that generation of readers grew up, manga for teenagers, young adults, and adults began to emerge. By the 1980s, mainstream manga had largely been established as shonen-oriented (targeted at young male readers).

Japanese manga was initially serialized in weekly magazines, with one chapter released per week, and simultaneously published in tankōbon volumes. (Tankōbon refers to standalone collected editions of previously serialized chapters, making it convenient for readers to own a complete story.) With the rise of the internet, digital manga gradually became a mainstream format.
